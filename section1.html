Section 1: One Principle, Clearly Taught

Learning to stay on track and teach with clarity

The Problem
Drifting Between Principles

It happens often: you set out to teach one principle — say, the Restoration — but partway through, you find yourself explaining the Plan of Salvation, or jumping ahead to baptism. By the end, the investigator isn't sure what the lesson was actually about. Nothing was taught poorly — but nothing was taught clearly , either.

Core Skill
Find the One Sentence

Before you open your mouth to teach a principle, you should be able to say what it is in one sentence . Not a paragraph. Not three points. One sentence that captures the heart of it. Everything you teach in that moment should support that sentence — and nothing else.

Try it below. Pick a principle, then write your one sentence.

Restoration: The fulness of Christ's gospel, lost through apostasy, has been brought back to the earth through the Prophet Joseph Smith.
Plan of Salvation: God has a plan that explains where we came from, why we are here, and where we can go after this life — and that plan centers on Jesus Christ.
Gospel of Jesus Christ: Through faith in Jesus Christ, repentance, baptism, receiving the Holy Ghost, and enduring to the end, we can return to live with God.
Repentance: Because of Jesus Christ, we can change, be forgiven, and become more like Him — repentance is a gift, not a punishment.

Watch For This
Signs You're Drifting

Check any of these you've noticed yourself doing while teaching:

Starting to explain a second principle before finishing the first
Answering a question by introducing a whole new topic
Using scriptures from three different principles in one explanation
Realizing at the end of the lesson you're not sure what the investigator actually learned

If you checked two or more, this section is especially for you — and that's a good thing. Awareness is the first step.

Role-Play Scenario
Teach the One Sentence

Read through this short scenario. Notice how the missionary stays anchored to one idea, even when the investigator's question could lead somewhere else.

Investigator: So if Joseph Smith brought back the true church, does that mean my church is wrong? My grandmother was baptized in it for 60 years.

Missionary: That's a fair question, and I respect your grandmother's faith deeply. Here's the simple truth I want to share: over time, after the original apostles died, parts of Christ's teachings and authority were lost from the earth — not because people were bad, but because that's what was prophesied would happen. The Restoration means God brought that fulness back through a prophet, so it's available again today.

Investigator: Okay... but what happens to people who were never part of that?

Missionary: That's a really good question — and it connects to another principle we'd love to share with you another time. For today, I just want to make sure this main idea is clear: God restored His full gospel through a prophet. Does that make sense so far?

Notice: the missionary didn't ignore the question. They acknowledged it, then gently parked it for later and returned to the one sentence for today's principle.

An investigator asks: "If God loves everyone, why do some people never even hear about this church in their whole lives?" — while you're teaching the Plan of Salvation. Write a short response that acknowledges the question but returns to your one sentence.

Takeaway
Before You Teach

Before every lesson, ask yourself: "What is the one thing I want this person to walk away understanding?" Write it down if you need to. When questions or tangents come up — and they will — acknowledge them warmly, then return to that one thing. Clarity isn't about saying less. It's about saying what matters most, clearly.
