Sections 1-4 Instructor Guide

How to lead missionaries through the four foundational teaching skills in a virtual meeting. Each section includes objectives, timing, discussion questions, role-play scenarios, debrief prompts, and instructor notes.

How to Use This Guide

  • This guide covers the Foundations Training Module (Sections 1-4: teaching skills) — not the PMG lessons. For PMG lessons (Lessons 1-4), use the separate Instructor Guide.
  • Each section is designed for one virtual meeting of 45-60 minutes. You can also combine two shorter sections into one meeting.
  • Missionaries should have their student section file open during the meeting (section1.html, section2.html, etc.) so they can refer to exercises as you run them together.
  • The pattern for each section: Open with prayer → state today's focus → discussion question → teach key point briefly → role-play in pairs → group debrief → application commitment → close with testimony and prayer.

Meeting Pattern (50-60 minutes)

Time Activity
0-5 min Opening prayer. State focus: "Today we're working on [Section X] because missionaries who master this will teach more clearly and help investigators feel the Spirit more."
5-15 min Discussion question. Hear 3-4 responses. Briefly teach the core concept (don't lecture — pull from responses).
15-35 min Role-play in pairs. Run 2 rounds: first try, feedback, second try. Rotate partners if time allows.
35-48 min Group debrief. Ask the debrief questions. Surface what went well and what to improve.
48-57 min Application commitment: "What is one thing you'll do differently in your very next teaching visit?"
57-60 min Closing testimony on today's skill + prayer.

Section 1: One Principle, Clearly Taught

45-60 min meeting
Student file: section1.html
▸
Meeting Objective
By the end of this meeting, every missionary should be able to identify the one core idea in any principle, teach it in one sentence, and recognize when they are drifting — and know how to return without losing the investigator.
Opening Discussion (10 min)
"Think of a lesson you taught recently where you felt like things went off track. What happened, and where did you lose the thread?" After 3-4 responses, ask: "What was the one thing you were actually trying to help the investigator understand in that lesson? Could you say it in one sentence right now?" 👁 Watch for: missionaries who describe drift as the investigator's fault ("they kept asking questions"). Gently redirect — drift is usually the missionary introducing a second principle or over-answering a tangential question.
Teach the Concept (5 min)
  • Before any lesson visit, a missionary should be able to complete this sentence: "The one thing I want [name] to understand today is ________."
  • Everything taught in that visit should support that one sentence — and nothing else.
  • When a question or tangent comes up, acknowledge it warmly, park it, and return to the sentence. This is a skill, not a personality trait. It can be practiced.
Role-Play: The One-Sentence Anchor (15 min)
Setup

Assign Missionary A one principle (e.g., Restoration, Repentance, or the Plan of Salvation). Before starting, Missionary A must write their one-sentence core message — they show it to the instructor but not to Missionary B.

Missionary A teaches for 90 seconds. Missionary B (investigator) then asks a question that could pull the lesson off course — choose one: (a) "But what about people who never heard of Joseph Smith?" (b) "What about the Trinity?" (c) "Does this mean my old church is wrong?"

Missionary A must acknowledge the question, park it warmly ("That's a great question — I'd love to talk about that — but today I really want to make sure this one thing is clear first..."), and return to their one sentence.

Debrief the Group Did the missionary stay anchored to their one sentence, or did they start teaching the question? Was the parking warm and natural, or did it feel dismissive? Did the investigator feel heard even though the question was set aside?
Run this twice: let Missionary A try once, give brief feedback, then run again with the same or a different question. The second try should be noticeably better. If it isn't, that's your key teaching moment for the group.
Role-Play 2: The Drift Self-Check (10 min)
Setup

This time, give Missionary A permission to drift on purpose — to start on one principle and slide into a second within 2 minutes. Missionary B observes and raises a hand the moment they feel like a second principle has started.

Pause when Missionary B raises their hand. Ask Missionary A: "Where did you feel the drift start? What could you have said to stay on track?"

Debrief Could the missionary identify their own drift moment? The ability to self-correct mid-lesson is the ultimate goal — not just performing well in practice.
Drift often starts with a well-meaning "and this connects to..." — that phrase is the warning signal. Help missionaries recognize it in themselves.
Application Commitment

Before the next teaching visit, each missionary writes their one-sentence core message for that lesson on paper and keeps it in front of them. They report back next meeting: did they stay anchored? Where did drift occur?

Section 2: Questions That Open the Heart

50-60 min meeting
Student file: section2.html
▸
Meeting Objective
By the end of this meeting, every missionary should be able to ask one simple, focused question at a time, recognize when they are stacking questions, and sit comfortably in silence after asking.
Opening Discussion (10 min)
"What's a question you've asked an investigator that you felt didn't land well — they seemed confused or gave a very short answer. What do you think happened?" After responses, ask: "What do you think they needed in that moment — a different question, or more silence after the one you asked?" 👁 Watch for: missionaries who think the problem was the investigator's unwillingness to talk, rather than the complexity of the question or the lack of silence after asking. The most common cause of short answers is a compound question or a too-quickly-filled silence.
Teach the Concept (5 min)
  • A simple question asks about one thing: a feeling, a thought, or a reaction. Not three things at once.
  • After asking, stop talking. Count silently to seven before saying anything. Silence is not emptiness — it's where the investigator does the real work.
  • Use D&C 11:21 (for missionaries): "Seek not to declare my word, but first seek to obtain my word" — understanding the person before teaching them is how the Spirit teaches through you.
Role-Play 1: Simple vs. Compound (15 min)
Setup

Give Missionary A a compound question to simplify before asking it (write it on the screen or read it aloud): "Do you think the Book of Mormon could be true, and if it is, what would that mean for your life, and are you willing to find out?"

Missionary A rewrites it as one simple question, then asks it to Missionary B. Missionary B answers naturally. Missionary A must wait 7 full seconds after Missionary B finishes before saying anything.

Debrief Was the rewritten question truly simple — one idea? Did the missionary fill the silence too quickly? How did Missionary B feel being given real space to think?
The silence is often the hardest part. Most missionaries have never practiced sitting in 7 seconds of quiet on a call or visit. Run the silence drill from the student guide as a group — count out loud together — before the role-play.
Role-Play 2: Follow the Investigator (15 min)
Setup

Missionary A reads a short passage from the Book of Mormon to Missionary B (e.g., Enos 1:4-5), then asks one simple question: "What stood out to you in that?"

Whatever Missionary B says, Missionary A must ask a follow-up question that directly builds on what was said — not a prepared question, but a genuine response to the answer received.

This continues for 3 exchanges. Missionary A may not introduce any new topic.

Debrief Did the conversation feel like an interview or a dialogue? Did Missionary A listen to the answers or wait to ask the next prepared question? What happened when they truly followed the investigator's thread?
This role-play reveals a deeper skill gap: missionaries who aren't really listening. If Missionary A's follow-up questions don't connect to what Missionary B actually said, that's the coaching point — listening, not questioning technique alone.
Application Commitment

In the next teaching visit, each missionary commits to asking no more than one question at a time, and counting silently to seven after each answer before responding. They bring one specific moment from that visit to share at the next meeting.

Section 3: Commitments That Lead to Christ

50-60 min meeting
Student file: section3.html
▸
Meeting Objective
By the end of this meeting, every missionary should be able to extend a commitment that flows directly from the principle taught, respond to hesitation warmly and without pressure, and plan a specific follow-up before leaving the lesson.
Opening Discussion (10 min)
"Think of a commitment you extended that the investigator agreed to, but didn't keep by the next visit. Looking back — why do you think it didn't happen?" After responses, ask: "When you extended that commitment, did it feel like a natural next step from what you'd just taught — or more like an add-on at the end?" 👁 Watch for: missionaries who blame investigators for not keeping commitments without examining the quality of the invitation or the follow-up plan. Both matter as much as the investigator's willingness.
Teach the Concept (5 min)
  • A good commitment grows directly from the principle just taught. Ask: "What is the most natural next step for someone who just understood this principle?"
  • A commitment without a follow-up plan is easy to forget. Before leaving, agree on a specific day and time to check in — not "soon."
  • Hesitation is normal. Acknowledge it, lower the bar if needed, and lock in the follow-up. Don't skip the follow-up just because the person seemed unsure.
Role-Play 1: Match the Commitment to the Principle (15 min)
Setup

Instructor assigns Missionary A one principle (e.g., Repentance, the Plan of Salvation, or the Book of Mormon). Missionary A teaches the principle in 60 seconds, then extends a commitment.

The group evaluates: does the commitment grow directly from the principle, or does it feel disconnected? (e.g., teaching Repentance and ending with "will you attend church" is disconnected; ending with "will you pray this week about one thing you'd like Christ to help you change" is connected.)

Debrief Could the group see a clear connection between the principle and the commitment? If not, what would a better-connected commitment look like?
The most common error is always ending with the same two or three generic commitments regardless of what was taught. Help missionaries see that every principle has its own natural invitation.
Role-Play 2: Handling Hesitation and Locking In Follow-Up (15 min)
Setup

Missionary A extends a commitment. Missionary B (investigator) responds with hesitation — assigned by the instructor: (a) "I'm not sure I'm ready for that yet," (b) "I'll try," or (c) "What if I can't do it perfectly?"

Missionary A must: (1) acknowledge the hesitation warmly, (2) lower the bar to something achievable if needed, and (3) agree on a specific day and time to follow up before closing the visit.

Debrief Did Missionary A acknowledge the hesitation without dismissing it? Did they lower the bar without abandoning the commitment entirely? Did they lock in a specific follow-up time?
The follow-up is what most missionaries skip — especially on a call or virtual visit. It feels awkward to schedule a check-in. But it's the most important moment in the commitment. Practice it until it feels natural.
Application Commitment

In the next teaching visit, each missionary must (1) extend a commitment that connects to the principle taught, and (2) agree on a specific follow-up time before the visit ends. They report back on both at the next meeting.

Section 4: The Heart of Each Lesson

50-60 min meeting
Student file: section4.html
▸
Meeting Objective
By the end of this meeting, every missionary should be able to state the core message of each of the four lesson areas in one sentence, and build a complete lesson moment (core message + simple question + natural commitment) for any principle on the spot.
Opening Discussion (10 min)
"If an investigator could only remember one thing from each of the four lessons — Restoration, Plan of Salvation, Gospel of Jesus Christ, Lifelong Discipleship — what would you want them to remember? Don't think about it too long. Just say it." Write the answers on screen or on paper as missionaries give them. Compare: are the answers consistent across the group? Are they the actual core, or are they details? 👁 Watch for: missionaries whose "one thing" is a doctrinal detail rather than a life-changing truth. If someone says "that Joseph Smith translated the gold plates" for Restoration, help them find the deeper core: "that God speaks again today through a living prophet and has restored His full truth."
Teach the Concept (5 min)
  • Every principle, and every lesson, has a heart — the ONE thing an investigator must feel and understand for that principle to do its work in their life.
  • Everything else in that lesson supports that heart. Questions, scriptures, and commitments all point back to it.
  • When a missionary knows the heart, they can teach with freedom and flexibility — because they know what they can leave out without losing anything essential.
Activity: One-Sentence Challenge (10 min)
Group Activity (no partner needed)

Instructor calls out a lesson area. Every missionary writes their one-sentence core message independently — no talking, 60 seconds.

Instructor picks 3-4 to read aloud. Group evaluates: Is it one sentence? Does it capture the heart, or a detail? Is it something that could change someone's life if they understood it?

Run this for all four lesson areas in under 10 minutes.

Debrief Which lesson areas produced the strongest one-sentence summaries? Which produced the most variation? That variation shows where the group's doctrinal foundation is least clear — and what to focus on in future meetings.
This activity also functions as a quick assessment. Strong missionaries will write clear, life-centered sentences. Weaker missionaries will write doctrinal facts or lists. Use this data to identify who needs more foundational support.
Role-Play: Build a Complete Lesson Moment (20 min)
Setup

Instructor assigns each missionary pair a principle from any of the four lessons. They have 3 minutes to prepare together: (1) their one-sentence core message, (2) one simple question they'll ask, (3) a natural commitment that flows from it.

They then "teach" it to the group in 2 minutes. The group watches for all three elements: Is the core message clear? Is the question simple and focused? Does the commitment connect to the principle?

Debrief Did all three elements hold together as one coherent moment? Or did the question feel disconnected from the core, or the commitment feel generic? Name the strongest example from the group and explain why it worked.
This role-play is the capstone of the entire four-section module. It integrates everything: the one-sentence anchor (Section 1), the simple question and silence (Section 2), and the natural commitment with follow-up (Section 3). If missionaries can do this well for any principle, they have internalized the framework.
Closing Testimony and Application

Invite missionaries to share which section or skill from Sections 1-4 has already made a difference in their teaching. Then close with testimony — not just of the framework, but of the principles themselves. The goal of all of this is that investigators come closer to Jesus Christ. End there.

If time allows, this is the meeting to assign missionaries to teach a full simulated lesson using all four skills together — either in pairs during personal study time, or as the basis for the next virtual meeting. The framework only becomes real in the field.
