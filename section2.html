Section 2: Questions That Open the Heart

Asking simply, then making space to listen

The Problem
Questions That Overwhelm

A question is meant to open a door. But often, without realizing it, missionaries stack two or three questions into one breath — or ask something so layered that the investigator doesn't know where to begin. The result? Silence, confusion, or a short "I don't know" that ends the conversation instead of deepening it.
"Seek not to declare my word, but first seek to obtain my word, and then shall your tongue be loosed; then, if you desire, you shall have my Spirit and my word, yea, the power of God unto the convincing of men." D&C 11:21

Good questions invite the Spirit to teach through the conversation — but only if there's room for the Spirit to work. That room is created by simplicity and pauses.

The Master Example
The Savior at the Well

"Jesus answered and said unto her, If thou knewest the gift of God, and who it is that saith to thee, Give me to drink, thou wouldest have asked of him, and he would have given thee living water." John 4:10

Notice the Savior's pattern with the woman at the well: He started with something simple and immediate — a request for water — and let the conversation unfold naturally from there, one idea at a time. He didn't open with "Do you believe in the Messiah, and have you considered your past, and would you be willing to change your life?" He met her where she was.

Core Skill
Ask, Then Wait

"And when ye shall receive these things, I would exhort you that ye would ask God, the Eternal Father, in the name of Christ, if these things are not true... and he will manifest the truth of it unto you." Moroni 10:4

Moroni's promise itself models the pattern: ask sincerely, then wait for the answer to come. When you ask an investigator a question, the silence that follows isn't empty — it's the space where they think, feel, and often where the Spirit speaks. Many missionaries fill that silence too quickly, out of nervousness, and the moment is lost.

Try sitting with a pause. Press start, and don't speak (or type) until it ends.
0:05

Role-Play Scenario
One Question, Real Silence

Missionary: We just read in Enos chapter 1 about how Enos prayed for a long time before he felt an answer. What stood out to you in that story?

[Pause — 6 seconds of silence]

Investigator: I guess... I liked that he didn't give up. I feel like I give up on things pretty fast.

Missionary: Thank you for sharing that. What do you think it would look like for you to not give up — in this?

"And my soul hungered; and I kneeled down before my Maker, and I cried unto him in mighty prayer and supplication for mine own soul; and all the day long did I cry unto him." Enos 1:4

One question. A real pause. A simple follow-up that builds on what the investigator said — not a new direction.

Practice
Simplify the Question

Here's a compound question a missionary might ask: "Do you think the Book of Mormon could be true, and if it is, what would that mean for your life, and are you willing to find out?"

Takeaway
Before You Ask

"Ask, and it shall be given you; seek, and ye shall find; knock, and it shall be opened unto you." Matthew 7:7

One question at a time. Then stop talking. The pause is not a gap to fill — it's where understanding takes root. Trust it.
